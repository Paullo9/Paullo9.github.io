Abstract

Large-scale measurements of local crystallographic orientation, often referred to as 4D-STEM, open up new avenues in electron microscopy. In this technique, a two-dimensional (2D) array of positions in real-space is scanned, and a two-dimensional diffraction pattern, typically a CBED pattern, is recorded for each of these positions; hence the name 4D-STEM. [[@ophusRecordingUsing4DSTEM2014]]

This is mainly feasible due to advances in computational power and modern direct electron detectors (DEDs) with increased dynamic range, reduced noise, and shorter read-out times compared to traditional detection techniques such as scintillator-coupled cameras. However, DEDs are expensive and are not readily available in many institutions. This work demonstrates the successful use of a conventional scintillator-based Gatan Continuum CMOS camera for the acquisition of 4D STEM data. The camera has a sensor format of $2048 \times 2048$ with 18-micron pixels and a frame rate of $94.2$ fps without binning. Compared to state-of-the-art DEDs, scintillator-based cameras have slower readout speeds and generate larger data sets. These can be hundreds of gigabytes in size and require specialized hardware and software to analyze. In view of these challenges, the experimental parameters such as convergence angle, step size, camera length, etc. were first optimized iteratively for the measurements. Subsequently, a workflow for processing large data sets was developed using **py4DSTEM**, an open source Python library to analyze 4D-STEM data sets, on a HPC cluster. [[@ophusQuantitativeScanningTransmission2023]]

The experiments focused on the investigation of gold nanoparticles and a high-pressure torsion (HPT) Ni-Cu alloy. Using **py4DSTEM**, images were simulated using virtual detectors, including bright field (BF), annular dark-field (ADF) and off-axis dark field (OADF) images, providing insights into the grain size, structure, and orientation of the samples. In addition, a script-based ACOM was implemented to automatically generate orientation mappings using a template matching procedure. This work demonstrates the effectiveness of HPC and script-based analysis for the computational challenges of large 4D-STEM data sets.
